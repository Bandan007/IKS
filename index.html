ECO ENGINEERING IN INDIAN ARCHITECTURE: NATURAL COOLING SYSTEMS IN HAVELIS AND TEMPLES

Sustainable Design | Climate-Responsive Architecture | Heritage Wisdom

For centuries, people in India built havelis and temples with a natural sense of comfort in mind. They knew how to shape spaces so air could move gently through them, keeping rooms cool even in strong summer heat. The same design choices also created soft, pleasant echoes that added to the atmosphere of daily life and worship. These buildings remind us that smart, climate-friendly ideas aren’t new—they’ve been part of our traditions all along.

Key Points:
  • Energy Efficiency: Reduces indoor temperature by 6-8°C naturally
  • Sustainability: Zero carbon emissions, uses local materials
  • Cultural Heritage: Blends ancient wisdom with modern architecture

METHODS - NATURAL COOLING SYSTEMS

A. Architectural Design Strategies

1

Courtyard System(Chowk):

  • Courtyards acted as the building’s cooling core, creating a shaded microclimate and using the stack effect to move hot air upward and fresh air inward.
2

Thick Walls and Thermal Mass:

  • Thick stone or brick walls absorbed daytime heat and released it slowly at night, keeping indoor temperatures stable and comfortable.
3

Strategic Orientation and Planning:

  • Havelis were oriented to avoid harsh sunlight, with main rooms facing north or east while service zones shielded living spaces from western heat.

B. Ventilation Techniques

1

Wind Catchers (Badgir/Malqaf):

  • Tall vertical shafts captured cooler high-level winds and funneled them indoors, sometimes passing air over water to enhance cooling.
2

Jaali Work (Perforated Screens):

  • Intricate stone or wooden jaalis filtered sunlight, improved ventilation, and reduced heat while maintaining privacy and diffusing glare.
3

High Ceilings and Domes:

  • High ceilings and ventilated domes pulled hot air upward and out, creating strong convection currents and preventing heat from settling indoors.

C. Water-Based Cooling Systems

1

Water-Based Cooling Systems(Baolis & Underground Channels):

  • Step wells and underground water channels cooled surrounding air, sending naturally chilled airflow into interior spaces through vents.

2

Fountains and Water Features:

  • Shallow fountains and flowing water increased evaporative cooling in courtyards, lowering temperatures and adding a calming visual effect.

3

Wet Screens and Khus Tatties:

  • Moistened vetiver screens cooled incoming air through evaporation while releasing a natural fragrance, improving comfort cheaply and effectively.

D. Material Selection and Surface Treatment

1

Reflective Surfaces:

  • Light-colored lime plaster and marble reflected sunlight, reducing heat absorption, with lime wash refreshed seasonally for maximum effect.
2

Local Materials:

  • Regional materials like sandstone, laterite, and granite naturally suited their climates, offering inherent thermal comfort and durability.
3

Green Roofs and Terraces:

  • Terrace vegetation cooled rooftops through evapotranspiration, while wet mud layers provided additional evaporative cooling.

Content:

E. Shading Devices

1

Deep Verandahs (Chajjas):

  • Deep verandahs, balconies, and overhangs blocked intense summer sun while still allowing winter sunlight to warm interior spaces.

2

Vegetation and Landscaping:

  • Strategically placed trees shaded building edges and courtyards, reducing heat buildup, with deciduous varieties offering seasonal temperature balance.
